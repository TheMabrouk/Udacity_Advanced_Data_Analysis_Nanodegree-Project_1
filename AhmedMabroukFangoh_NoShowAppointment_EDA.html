Introduction¶

This document will report on the No Show Appointments dataset, which includes 110527 records and 14 features of appointments from Brazil.

Main Question: What features would be required to predict the likelihood of a patient showing up to their appointment?

Further Exploratory Questions:

  1. Would a patient's enrollment in the social welfare program Bolsa Famlia affect their likelihood to show up
  2. How do the age groups and genders compare in likelihood to show up
  3. For which medical conditions are patients more commited to showing up
  4. Do notifications by SMS help the patient attend their appointments

Items of note:

  • Features PatientId, AppointmentID, AppointmentDay & ScheduledDay will not be used for analysis.
  • Age has -1 value which is impossible and will be dropped.
  • AppointmentDay is a string, which would make analysis inefficient. There are no time values, so the format will be changed to display date only.
  • Scholarship, Hipertension, Diabetes, Alcoholism, & SMS_received will be converted to boolean.
  • No-show can be confusing. It will be renamed to Showed_Up and values will be inverted for readability and clarity. Its datatype will be converted to boolean.

Conclusions¶

  • For the purposes of predicting a patients likelihood to show up to an appointment:
    • Scholarship & SMS_received showed negative correlation to likelihood to show up.
    • Age, Hipertension, & Diabetes showed positive correlation to likelihood to show up.
    • Gender & Alchoholism were bad indicators for the likelihood to show up.
  • The analysis was limited by the following:
    • More details are required to explain the unusual results with the Scholarship & SMS_received features. It would be expected that they both show a positive correlation.
    • A more detailed analysis could be achieved if more features were provided, such as financial situation, distance from medical center, etc.
    • The dataset's samples tend to skew heavily towards patients without any of the stated medical conditions and to those of young/middle age, making conclusions for these two features not as accurate.
